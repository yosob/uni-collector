## Curriculum Summary

**Semester 1:**
- Objects, Spaces and Boundaries (20 credits) — Compulsory
- Research Methods (20 credits) — Compulsory
- Entrepreneurial Practice (10 credits) — Compulsory
- Collaborative Social Challenge Project (10 credits) — Compulsory

**Semester 2:**
- Photography and Publics (20 credits) — Compulsory
- Photographic Situations (20 credits) — Compulsory
- Final Major Project (Research and Planning) (30 credits) — Compulsory
- Collaborative Community Project (10 credits) — Compulsory

**Semester 3 (Summer):**
- Final Major Project (Production and Sharing) (30 credits) — Compulsory
- Collaborative Enterprise Project (10 credits) — Compulsory

This course offers an exciting opportunity for emerging practitioners in photography and its associated forms to develop their work via a series of key interrogations. The MA Photography course presents an exciting curriculum which emphasises support of your unique practice and how it relates to our professional and social responsibilities as image-makers. The course challenges traditional epistemologies of what the photograph is, asking students to question the interstices of photography and object, photography and space, as well as medium and genre boundaries. Students will explore the connection between the making and the making public of photographic work and processes, with the opportunity to develop and test their own publishing models. The course welcomes practitioners with diverse backgrounds and adopts an approach to photography which considers associated mediums and methodologies within an expanded field. Students may work with archives, produce installation pieces, publish, run collaborative projects, or manage photographic events and performances. 84% of graduates from the media cluster were in work or further study after 15 months (Graduate Outcomes Survey for 2020 leavers).

**Assessment methods:** Coursework, portfolio submissions, photographic projects, written assignments, research proposals, collaborative project presentations, final major project (body of photographic work with supporting research documentation).
