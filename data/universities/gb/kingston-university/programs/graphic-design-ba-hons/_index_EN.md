## Overview

On this course, you'll be encouraged to be innovative, invent and inspire change. In addition to learning core design and research skills, you'll work on live projects throughout the course. Recent collaborations have included RSA, D&AD, Creative Review, The Science Museum, Amnesty International, Croydon Council, The British Film Institute and Save the Children.

You'll develop a personal practice through rigorous investigation of a range of focused projects. Design fields include photography, film and animation, typography, interaction, service design, user experience, social design, digital, and book and editorial design.

At the end of the course, you'll be equipped with a combination of creative skills, cognitive processes and methodologies, which will help you to shape the future for business, culture and communities.

As part of Kingston School of Art, students benefit from joining a creative community where collaborative working and critical practice are encouraged. Workshops and labs are open to all disciplines, enabling students and staff to work together, share ideas and explore multi-disciplinary making.

The course has an applied approach to the expanding field of graphic design: you'll use studios and facilities to experiment, exploring new ways to push the boundaries of visual communication. Collaboration, teamwork and cross-disciplinary projects are strongly encouraged to reflect professional practice.

## Course Structure

### Year 1

Year 1 encourages an open-minded and exploratory approach to understanding design. You'll work on individual and team projects, to develop your knowledge of the design process, visual language, typographic principles and core skills.

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| History and Critical Study of Graphic Design | 30 |
| Design & Context | 30 |
| Visual Communication | 30 |
| Ideas and Innovation | 30 |

**History and Critical Study of Graphic Design** — An introduction to the history and critical study of graphic design. Students develop an understanding of the historical development of graphic design within the larger field of cultural production, and an awareness of core debates and concepts in the history of art and design. Through thematic sessions, students reflect on key stages in the history of graphic design; unpack images, objects and processes; and engage with ideas and practices that have shaped graphic design as a social, cultural and economic activity.

**Design & Context** — Introduces how the designer can engage, interact with and respond to different contexts and varied audiences, including the personal and professional, geographical and environmental, social and political, commercial and educational, local and global. Emphasis on developing experience and understanding of how design and communication function in the wider world.

**Visual Communication** — Introduces the fundamental principles and components of graphic design and visual communication through projects, workshops, experiments and exercises that explore the relationship between materials, methods, messages and meaning. Key areas include typography and layout, hierarchy and composition, visual thinking and visual language, process and production techniques, and context.

**Ideas and Innovation** — Focuses on the generation of concepts and ideas. Develops conceptual ability and creative confidence in responding to a range of design and communication challenges. Introduces different ways of thinking and generating ideas, with emphasis on currency, appropriateness and dynamism of approach and solution.

### Year 2

Year 2 helps you develop creative and interpersonal skills, used to explore imaginative ways to communicate. You'll develop a particular area of graphic design through self-initiated projects, complementing your studio work. You'll learn about the wide social and cultural context in which graphic designers work, and will collaborate with students from other courses on projects.

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Design Process | 30 |
| Theorising Graphic Design (Critical and Historical Studies) | 30 |
| Positioning Portfolio | 30 |
| Communication & Dissemination | 30 |

**Design Process** — Explores and understands the design process, with particular focus on the relationship between research, development and the successful realisation of ideas. Introduces a range of methodologies including design thinking, prototyping and iteration. Projects extend design vocabularies and knowledge of materials and visual languages (including typography, photography and moving image).

**Theorising Graphic Design (Critical and Historical Studies)** — Builds on historical and thematic content from Year 1 and emphasises the theorisation of contemporary graphic design practice. Lectures, seminars, workshops, tutorials, screenings and visits inform and support emerging research interests and the development of independent visual and academic research skills.

**Positioning Portfolio** — Develops understanding of the role of the designer and enables you to contextualise your personal design vision and ambition. Includes developing understanding of how to present work dynamically and appropriately, equipping you with the ability to develop and express opinions and adopt different perspectives. Culminates in the design of a positioning portfolio in preparation for Year 3.

**Communication & Dissemination** — Explores different modes of communication and dissemination, focusing on the interactive nature of graphic design and visual communications. Projects encourage experimentation, embracing risk and developing innovative approaches and solutions, including opportunities for interdisciplinary projects and participatory practice.

### Optional Year

Many students take advantage of the ERASMUS scheme to spend a year studying or working in Europe. The course has a network of links with prestigious design institutions. The study abroad programme with links in Japan, Korea and the US also supports students studying internationally.

### Year 3 (Final Year)

In your final year, you'll work from a range of briefs, and develop your own, to reflect your personal interests and career aspirations. Some students work across a range of areas; others choose to specialise in one — this could be branding, social design, editorial design, film and moving image, photography, advertising and promotion, typography or interactive design.

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Extended Research Project | 15 |
| Creative Design | 30 |
| Professional Practice & Portfolio | 60 |
| Future Skills | 15 |

**Extended Research Project** — Supports an extended research project that deepens understanding of critical issues relevant to practice. You'll explore a topic of your choosing, framed by questions emerging from your own design work and informed by relevant theory, history or cultural context.

**Creative Design** — Concerned with the continued exploration and refinement of techniques, themes and tools that inform an innovative approach to idea generation and insight gathering. Emphasis on completion of a significant body of creative work that strategically reflects the individual's interests, achievements and ambition.

**Professional Practice & Portfolio** — Supports the practical and strategic development of a professional body of work that reflects the consolidation of an individual's design practice. Provides the opportunity to identify personal attributes and strengths, critically edit and select from project work, and manage and refine work for effective portfolio presentation across a range of professional formats. Includes preparation for employment or postgraduate study, informed by industry professional talks and seminars.

**Future Skills** — A dedicated opportunity to develop Future Skills Graduate Attributes. You'll self-assess your current skills profile, determine which attributes and skills you need to develop to support your career ambitions, supported by a dedicated career coach. Activities may include short courses, enrichment activities, micro-placements and experiential learning.

### Portfolio Requirements

Please submit a portfolio in digital format, 15–20 pages in length. We're looking for you to demonstrate four key values:

- Questioning
- Curiosity
- Technical ability
- Enthusiasm

A project may be spread over several pages or take up just one. Combining your work into a PDF is recommended. You can include links to a website, Instagram, Flickr or Vimeo account.

## Teaching & Assessment

Teaching is delivered through a combination of:

- Studio-based projects and practical workshops
- Live projects with industry partners
- Lectures, seminars and tutorials
- Group critiques and peer review
- Visits to studios, events and conferences (UK and international)

Assessment typically comprises:

- Practical work (presentations, exhibitions)
- Coursework (essays, reports, self-assessment, portfolios, dissertation)
