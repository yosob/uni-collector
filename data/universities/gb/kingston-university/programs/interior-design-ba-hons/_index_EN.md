## Overview

On this course, you'll be encouraged to be innovative, invent and inspire change as you explore the reuse of existing buildings and interior spaces. The course focuses on the rehabilitation and re-use of existing structures, developing projects at a variety of scales from temporary pop-ups to building transformations.

You'll work on live projects developed collaboratively with entrepreneurs, developers, community groups and some of the best design studios in the UK. Taught by experts including designers, architects and engineers, you'll develop skills in problem-solving, speculation and material innovation, exploring projects such as floating community spaces or retail reimaginings.

As part of Kingston School of Art, students benefit from joining a creative community where collaborative working and critical practice are encouraged. You'll work in newly refurbished, purpose-built design studios and have access to a dedicated art, design and material library and impressive modelling and fabrication facilities.

The course has been running for many years and is one of the longest established degree programmes dealing with interior design in the United Kingdom. Throughout the course, emphasis is placed on large-scale work where materials and detail must be considered, and students are encouraged to develop individual and imaginative work while ensuring appropriate professional and technical knowledge.

## Course Structure

### Year 1

Year 1 introduces key principles of interior design. The Design Studies strand concentrates on issues such as form, proportion, structure, colour and light, culminating in a small-scale interior design project. Essential communication skills and the historical context of the subject are also introduced.

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Design Process: Context | 30 |
| Design Communication | 30 |
| History of Designed Spaces | 30 |
| Workshop and Studio | 30 |

**Design Process: Context** — Introduces the full interior design process in context. Students explore fundamental principles of interior design including form, proportion, structure, colour and light through a series of studio-based design projects. Initial work is synthesised to demonstrate understanding through a small-scale interior design project.

**Design Communication** — Introduces the principles of ideation and communication in interior design. Essential communication skills concerning drawing, model making, digital imaging and presentation are developed to equip students to undertake design project work.

**History of Designed Spaces** — Introduces a thematic history of designed spaces, exploring the historical context of the subject. Students develop understanding of how designed interiors have evolved across different periods and cultures, providing a foundation for critical engagement with the discipline.

**Workshop and Studio** — Workshop and studio function as integrated creative environments. Students gain hands-on experience with materials, processes and technologies relevant to interior design, developing practical skills that support design project work.

### Year 2

Year 2 provides an opportunity to apply principles introduced in Year 1 to a range of interior design projects. Design work begins with the investigation of various sites and throughout the year explores aspects of interior design at increasingly larger scales, from spatial organisations to the resolution of specific details at full size. Collaborative and interdisciplinary work with students from other design disciplines is a key feature.

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Interior Practice: Design | 30 |
| Interior Practice: Adaptive Reuse | 30 |
| Interior Practice: Narrative | 30 |
| Critical and Historical Studies | 30 |

**Interior Practice: Design** — Develops contemporary design practice through the investigation of various sites. Students explore aspects of interior design at increasingly larger scales, ranging from the development of spatial organisations to the resolution of specific details at full size. Collaborative and interdisciplinary work with students from other design disciplines (such as graphics, fashion and product & furniture design) is encouraged.

**Interior Practice: Adaptive Reuse** — Focuses on the adaptive reuse and rehabilitation of existing buildings. Students engage with real sites and develop design proposals that respond to the character and potential of existing structures, addressing issues of sustainability and conservation.

**Interior Practice: Narrative** — Explores spatial storytelling, scenography and exhibition design. Students develop the ability to create immersive environments and experiential spaces that communicate narratives through the manipulation of space, light, materials and objects.

**Critical and Historical Studies** — Builds on the historical and thematic content introduced at Level 4, exploring contemporary aspects of the subject in relation to other art and design areas. Students develop research skills and critical debate relating to interior design practice.

### Optional Year

Many students take advantage of the Erasmus programme to study or work in Europe for a year. The course has strong international links and opportunities for overseas field study visits and educational exchange programmes.

### Year 3 (Final Year)

In your final year, you'll manipulate your understanding of the first two years of the course towards the development of design project work of particular personal interest. The course investigates professional practice issues that enable you to locate your design work in a professional context.

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Interior Practice: Research & Design | 30 |
| Major Design Project | 60 |
| Extended Research Project | 15 |
| Future Skills | 15 |

**Interior Practice: Research & Design** — Continues from Level 5, providing students with an opportunity to develop design project work of particular personal interest. Professional practice issues are investigated, enabling students to locate their design work in a professional context while introducing the design world beyond the course.

**Major Design Project** — A comprehensive, self-directed design project that represents the culmination of the student's learning journey. Students develop a significant body of work that demonstrates their individual approach to interior design, from initial concept through to detailed resolution.

**Extended Research Project** — Supports an extended research project that deepens understanding of critical issues relevant to practice. Students explore a topic of their choosing, framed by questions emerging from their own design work and informed by relevant theory, history or cultural context.

**Future Skills** — A dedicated opportunity to develop Future Skills Graduate Attributes. Students self-assess their current skills profile, determine which attributes and skills they need to develop to support their career ambitions, supported by a dedicated career coach. Activities may include short courses, enrichment activities, micro-placements and experiential learning.

## Teaching & Assessment

Teaching is delivered through a combination of:

- Studio-based design projects and practical workshops
- Live projects with industry partners and community groups
- Lectures, seminars and tutorials
- Group critiques and peer review
- Site visits, field trips and study visits (UK and international)
- Interdisciplinary collaboration with other design disciplines

Assessment typically comprises:

- Practical work (presentations, exhibitions, portfolio submissions)
- Coursework (essays, reports, self-assessment, dissertation)
