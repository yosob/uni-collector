## Overview

The Animation MA at Kingston School of Art is designed for students who want to push the boundaries of animation practice. The course takes an experimental and creative approach, encouraging you to develop a distinctive personal voice in animation.

You'll explore a wide range of animation techniques — from traditional drawn animation and stop-motion to CGI and experimental moving image — while developing critical thinking and research skills. The course balances practical production with theoretical and contextual studies, enabling you to create work that is both conceptually rigorous and technically accomplished.

Based at the Knights Park campus, you'll have access to dedicated animation studios, stop-frame facilities, digital labs and specialist software. Kingston School of Art fosters a collaborative, interdisciplinary environment where animation students work alongside those in illustration, graphic design, fine art and other creative disciplines.

The course has strong links with industry, with visiting practitioners, guest lecturers and live project opportunities. Previous students have gone on to work at leading animation studios, set up independent practices, and screen their work at international film festivals.

## Course Structure

The Animation MA is structured around a series of core modules that build progressively towards a self-directed major project.

### Semester 1

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Animation Practice | 30 |
| Digital Arts & Moving Image | 30 |

**Animation Practice** — This module introduces you to the conceptual and practical frameworks for advanced animation practice. You'll experiment with a range of animation techniques, developing your creative voice and refining your technical skills. Projects encourage risk-taking and innovation, with emphasis on developing original content and distinctive visual languages.

**Digital Arts & Moving Image** — Explores the intersection of animation with broader digital arts and moving image practices. You'll engage with contemporary debates around digital culture, time-based media and visual storytelling, while developing technical proficiency in relevant software and production tools. The module encourages cross-disciplinary thinking and experimentation with emerging technologies.

### Semester 2

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Creative Process & Research Methods | 30 |
| Professional Practice | 30 |

**Creative Process & Research Methods** — Develops your research skills and critical understanding of creative processes. You'll engage with a range of qualitative and practice-led research methodologies, contextualising your animation practice within wider cultural, theoretical and historical frameworks. This module supports the development of your major project proposal.

**Professional Practice** — Focuses on the professional dimensions of animation practice. You'll explore the creative industries landscape, develop your understanding of funding, commissioning and distribution models, and build professional skills including pitching, project management and collaborative working. Visiting practitioners provide industry insights and networking opportunities.

### Semester 3 (Major Project)

| Module | Credits |
|--------|---------|
| Major Project | 60 |

**Major Project** — The culmination of the course, this self-directed module enables you to produce a substantial body of original animation work. You'll develop your project from initial concept through research, development and production to final presentation. The major project is supported by tutorials, group critiques and technical workshops. You may choose to produce a completed animated film, a series of experimental works, or a practice-based research project.

## Entry Requirements

| Qualification | Requirement |
|---------------|-------------|
| Undergraduate Degree | A good honours degree (2:2 or above) in animation, illustration, fine art, graphic design, film or a related discipline |
| Professional Experience | Applications from candidates with relevant professional experience and a strong portfolio will also be considered |

### Portfolio Requirements

You should submit a portfolio demonstrating:

- Evidence of animation or moving image work (showreel recommended, 2–3 minutes)
- Drawings, storyboards and visual development work
- Examples of creative exploration and experimentation
- Critical reflection on your practice

A showreel or links to online work (Vimeo, YouTube) should be included where possible.

## Teaching & Assessment

Teaching is delivered through:

- Studio-based workshops and practical sessions
- Individual and group tutorials
- Lectures, seminars and critiques
- Technical demonstrations and software training
- Guest lectures and industry visits
- Self-directed study and practice

Assessment typically comprises:

- Practical project work (animated films, showreels, installations)
- Written work (essays, research proposals, critical reflections)
- Presentations and pitches
- Portfolio submission

## Facilities

Students have access to specialist facilities at Knights Park campus:

- Dedicated animation studios
- Stop-motion animation stages
- Digital media labs with industry-standard software (Adobe Creative Suite, Toon Boom, Maya, Dragonframe)
- Rostrum camera facilities
- Sound recording and editing suite
- Rendering farm
- Printmaking and bookbinding workshops
- Photography and filmmaking studios
