overview: 'The MLitt in Art Writing is a one-year taught postgraduate programme based
  in the School of Fine Art at The Glasgow School of Art, with the degree awarded
  by the University of Glasgow. It is the only dedicated graduate programme of its
  kind in the UK and Europe. The programme is built on two principal elements: a unique
  understanding of art writing as an interdisciplinary studio practice, and project-based
  placements with local cultural organisations. Drawing from disciplines including
  visual art, art criticism, art history and literature, the programme involves creative,
  philosophical, critical and theoretical approaches to writing about art, writing
  with art and writing as art. Students develop their own creative-critical writing
  practices within a distinctive studio-based pedagogical model, working alongside
  MLitt Curatorial Practice and MLitt Fine Art Practice students.'

curriculum_summary: 'Stage 1 (PG Cert, 60 credits):

  - Writing Practice I (20 credits) — studio-based creative-critical writing development

  - Art Writing: Histories, Theories and Methodologies (20 credits) — lecture, seminar
  and reading group course

  - Core Research Methods: Research Methods and Methodologies in Practice (20 credits)
  — shared SoFA PGT/PGR course


  Stage 2 (PG Dip, 60 credits):

  - Writing Practice II (20 credits) — includes project placement with a local cultural
  organisation

  - The Genre-Bending Art of Essaying (20 credits) — delivered by School of Critical
  Studies, University of Glasgow

  - PGT Elective (20 credits) — self-selected from available postgraduate options


  Stage 3 (MLitt, 60 credits):

  - Writing Practice III: Masters Project (60 credits) — major research project, co-supervised
  by specialist SoFA practitioners and researchers


  Exit awards: PG Certificate after Stage 1, PG Diploma after Stage 2, MLitt after
  Stage 3.

  Teaching is studio-based with group critiques, workshops, individual tutorials and
  seminars. Students participate in cohort and cross-school critiques and the SoFA
