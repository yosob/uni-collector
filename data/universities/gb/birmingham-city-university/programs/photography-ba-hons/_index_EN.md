# Photography BA (Hons)

## Overview

BA (Hons) Photography at BCU is a practice-based course that develops your individual photographic voice through experimentation with photographic and moving image practices. The course provides foundations in visual communication, photographic principles, and enterprise, before encouraging you to identify your own creative direction through live projects, work placements, and collaborative practice. You will have access to industry-standard photographic studios, darkrooms, moving image equipment, and editing suites within the historic Birmingham School of Art building. Ranked top 10 in the UK for Film Production and Photography for three consecutive years (Guardian League Table 2024–2026), with 96% student satisfaction (NSS 2025).

## Course Structure

### First Year

Your first year provides the foundations of photographic and moving image practice. You will be introduced to visual communication principles, photographic techniques, and the enterprise of photography, alongside a chosen narrative pathway.

**Core Modules (100 credits):**

- **Introduction to Visual Communication** (20 credits) — Foundation module introducing key concepts and theories of visual communication as they apply to photographic practice.
- **Principles and Practice of Photography** (40 credits) — Core practical module developing fundamental photographic skills, techniques, and creative approaches across a range of photographic genres.
- **Enterprise of Photography** (20 credits) — Introduction to the business and professional aspects of photography, including freelance practice, client management, and self-promotion.
- **Discourse** (20 credits) — Critical and contextual studies module exploring the history, theory, and discourse surrounding photography and visual culture.

**Optional Modules (choose 20 credits):**

- **Narrative (Space and Place)** (20 credits) — Explore photographic narrative through the themes of space and place.
- **Narrative (Time and Sequence)** (20 credits) — Investigate the relationship between time, sequence, and narrative.
- **Narrative (Sound and Movement)** (20 credits) — Combine sound and movement with photography to create dynamic narrative works.
- **Narrative (Text and Image)** (20 credits) — Examine the interplay between text and image in photographic narratives.
- **Narrative (People and Identity)** (20 credits) — Explore themes of people and identity through portrait, documentary, and socially engaged practice.

### Second Year

Develop your individual direction through contextual exploration and practical experimentation, supported by live projects and professional opportunities.

**Core Modules (100 credits):**

- **Context of Photography** (40 credits) — Deepen your understanding of the cultural, social, and professional contexts in which photography operates.
- **Identifying Direction** (40 credits) — Begin to define and refine your individual photographic direction through sustained personal projects.
- **Live Project** (20 credits) — Work on a real-world brief set by an external client or organisation.

**Optional Modules (choose 20 credits):**

- **Work Placement** (20 credits) — Undertake a work placement in a relevant photographic or creative industry setting.
- **Collaborative Practice** (20 credits) — Collaborate with students from other disciplines on an interdisciplinary creative project.

### Professional Placement Year (Optional)

Spend a full year with an employer after Year 2. Fees are 20% of the full-time course fee for that year.

### Final Year

Focus on developing a sustained, in-depth body of work that demonstrates your individual creative identity and professional competence.

**Core Modules (120 credits):**

- **Critical Practice** (60 credits) — A major written research project contextualising your photographic practice within critical, theoretical, and historical frameworks.
- **Major Project** (60 credits) — Produce a substantial body of photographic work demonstrating your individual creative vision, technical mastery, and professional readiness.
